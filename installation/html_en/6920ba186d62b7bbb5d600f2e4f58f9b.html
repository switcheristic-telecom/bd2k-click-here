What you should know before you leave your child home alone

BY BARBARA TURVETT

Three afternoons a week, eleven-year-old Jordan Barr leaves a public elementary school in New York City, boards a school bus that lets him off six blocks from home, and walks a preset "safe" route by himself. He enters his building, greets the doorman, and rides the elevator up to the apartment he shares with his parents and nine-year-old sister. He takes out his key, unlocks the door, and enters, making sure the door is locked behind him. It's 3:15 P.M. and he'll be alone until 6:30, when his mom brings his sister home from ballet class. For the past year, Jordan has been a "latchkey child," one who cares for himself or is left with someone under age 15 for some time each week while his parents are working.

Jordan is one of the estimated 2.5 million latchkey children in the United States today — not just in cities, but in suburbs across the country, according to the National Child Care Survey (NCCS). Their numbers tell a story of changing demographics and limited choices. An increase in the number of working mothers, as well as single-parent families, combined with a decrease in extended families that once helped with child care, have contributed to the growing ranks of latchkey kids.

And while most researchers and child-rearing experts maintain that leaving children to fend for themselves for even short periods without adult supervision is the least appealing child-care choice, other options — such as day care, after-school activities, and baby-sitters — are often not logistically or economically feasible.

"Though we don't advocate that parents leave kids home alone — and never if a child is under age ten — we have to recognize the need is a modern reality," says Ginny Markell, vice president for programs for the National PTA. Markell, a teacher, a nurse, and a mother of two, has been working to develop child self-care strategies, based on parents' experiences, including her own. "My two boys, now both out of high school, were latchkey kids, and it worked fine for them," she says. "It can be a positive experience if parents are careful to provide clear guidelines, rules, and structure." If there's a latchkey child in your family, here's how to make sure she stays safe and happy.
